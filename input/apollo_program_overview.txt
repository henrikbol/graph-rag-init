The Apollo Program was a NASA spaceflight program that ran from 1961 to 1972. It was designed to land humans on the Moon and bring them safely back to Earth. The program was conceived during the presidency of Dwight D. Eisenhower but was formally announced by President John F. Kennedy on May 25, 1961, during a special address to Congress. Kennedy declared the ambitious goal of landing a man on the Moon before the end of the decade.

NASA, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, was responsible for managing the Apollo program. The agency worked closely with several major contractors. North American Aviation built the Command and Service Module, while Grumman Aircraft Engineering Corporation designed and manufactured the Lunar Module. The Saturn V rocket, the most powerful rocket ever built at the time, was developed under the direction of Wernher von Braun at the Marshall Space Flight Center in Huntsville, Alabama.

The program suffered a devastating setback on January 27, 1967, when a cabin fire during a launch rehearsal test for Apollo 1 killed astronauts Gus Grissom, Ed White, and Roger Chaffee at Cape Kennedy in Florida. The tragedy led to major design and engineering changes in the spacecraft and a twenty-month delay in crewed flights.

Apollo 7, launched on October 11, 1968, was the first crewed Apollo mission and successfully tested the Command Module in Earth orbit. Apollo 8, launched on December 21, 1968, became the first crewed spacecraft to leave low Earth orbit, reach the Moon, orbit it, and return safely. Astronauts Frank Borman, Jim Lovell, and William Anders became the first humans to see the far side of the Moon.

The culmination of the program came with Apollo 11 on July 20, 1969. Commander Neil Armstrong and Lunar Module Pilot Buzz Aldrin landed the Lunar Module Eagle on the Moon's Sea of Tranquility while Command Module Pilot Michael Collins orbited above. Armstrong became the first human to set foot on the lunar surface, followed by Aldrin approximately twenty minutes later.
