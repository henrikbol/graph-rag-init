Following the success of Apollo 11, NASA continued its lunar exploration with five more successful landing missions. Apollo 12, commanded by Pete Conrad with Alan Bean as Lunar Module Pilot and Richard Gordon as Command Module Pilot, launched on November 14, 1969. The mission demonstrated precision landing capability by touching down within walking distance of the Surveyor 3 probe that had landed on the Moon two years earlier.

Apollo 13 launched on April 11, 1970, with astronauts Jim Lovell, Jack Swigert, and Fred Haise. An oxygen tank explosion in the Service Module forced the crew to abandon the planned Moon landing and use the Lunar Module as a lifeboat. The mission became known as a successful failure due to the remarkable efforts of the crew and Mission Control in Houston, Texas, to bring the astronauts safely home.

Apollo 14, launched on January 31, 1971, saw astronaut Alan Shepard, the first American in space, walk on the Moon accompanied by Edgar Mitchell. Apollo 15, launched on July 26, 1971, was the first extended scientific expedition to the Moon. Commander David Scott and Lunar Module Pilot James Irwin spent three days on the lunar surface at the Hadley-Apennine region, using the first Lunar Roving Vehicle.

Apollo 16, launched on April 16, 1972, landed in the Descartes Highlands with astronauts John Young and Charles Duke conducting extensive geological surveys. The final mission, Apollo 17, launched on December 7, 1972. Commander Eugene Cernan and Lunar Module Pilot Harrison Schmitt, a trained geologist, spent over three days on the surface in the Taurus-Littrow valley. Cernan remains the last human to have walked on the Moon.

In total, twelve astronauts walked on the lunar surface during the Apollo program. The missions returned approximately 842 pounds of lunar samples and deployed numerous scientific instruments that continued transmitting data long after the astronauts departed.
